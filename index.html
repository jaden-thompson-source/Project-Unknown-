how driven-frequency screens work
DRIVEN FREQUENCY
The screen's controller broadcasts an AC signal at a fixed frequency into the sensor grid. It only looks for disturbances at that same frequency. Anything else is noise to it.
ACTIVE REJECTION
Premium screens (iPad, MPP) use narrow-band filters and ignore signals outside their window. A cheap rubber stylus touching at 0 Hz DC creates a disturbance at all frequencies — but Apple rejects the broadband smear and only accepts 250 kHz.
STYLUS DESIGN IMPACT
V1 must broadcast at the right frequency for the target screen. Wide-band screens (cheap Android) tolerate a range. Narrow-band screens (iPad) need a frequency-locked oscillator within ±15 kHz or detection drops to zero.
V2 OVERLAY BYPASS
V2 doesn't emit a frequency — it adds a transparent conductive overlay that modifies the existing electrostatic field the screen is already measuring. No frequency matching needed. Works on every screen, protocol-agnostic.
